Why FinStart Exists

Germany has a problem. Despite being one of the most economically sophisticated countries in the world, the average German has minimal exposure to equity markets. Roughly 85% of adults do not own a single share or ETF. The dominant approach to personal finance is to save — carefully, responsibly — and to be deeply suspicious of the stock market.

This is not irrational. German financial culture was shaped by historical hyperinflation, currency collapses, and deep institutional conservatism. The caution makes historical sense. But in the modern world of low-cost index funds and 7% historical returns, the cost of this caution is enormous — measured in tens of thousands of euros of compound growth never earned.

Schools do not teach this. Most personal finance content in German is either patronising, overly complex, or trying to sell you something. I looked for a resource that explained things simply, honestly, and specifically for young Germans starting from zero — and could not find one.

So I built it.

What FinStart Is — and Is Not

FinStart is a financial education platform. It explains concepts, shows you the mathematics behind investing, and gives you the knowledge to make informed decisions yourself. It is not a financial advisor. It does not tell you what to buy, when to buy it, or what your specific optimal portfolio is.

Everything on FinStart is educational. The model portfolios are examples used to illustrate principles — not personal recommendations. The news summaries provide context — not trading signals. The investor profile quiz generates a starting framework for thinking — not investment instructions.

I am 16. I am not a licensed financial advisor. What I am is someone who has spent a significant amount of time studying markets, investing, and financial concepts — and who wants to share that clearly.
