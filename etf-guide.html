What is an ETF?
The Complete Beginner's Guide

The simplest investment tool that exists — explained from the ground up. What it is, how it works, why it beats most alternatives, and how to start using one in Germany today.

📖 12 min read 🆓 Free 🇩🇪 Germany-specific

What Does ETF Actually Stand For?

ETF stands for Exchange Traded Fund. Let us break that down word by word, because each part tells you something important.

Exchange Traded means it is bought and sold on a stock exchange — the same way you would buy a share in a company like Apple or Volkswagen. You can buy and sell it at any point during market hours, and the price updates in real time throughout the day.

Fund means it is a pooled investment vehicle — a collection of many different assets bundled together into one single product that you buy as a unit.

Put those two together and you have a fund that trades on an exchange. Simple in concept — and remarkably powerful in practice.

The Key Question: What Is Inside an ETF?

Most ETFs are index funds. This means they track a specific index — a list of companies or assets grouped by certain criteria. An S&P 500 ETF, for example, holds tiny pieces of all 500 companies in the S&P 500 index. When you buy one share of the ETF, you instantly own a proportional slice of all 500 companies simultaneously.

How ETFs Actually Work

When you invest in an ETF, you are not buying individual shares in companies directly. You are buying units of a fund that holds those shares on your behalf. The ETF manager — a company like iShares (BlackRock), Vanguard, or Xtrackers — buys and holds all the underlying assets and issues units of the fund for investors to buy.

The ETF's job is simple: track the performance of its index as closely as possible. It does not try to pick the best companies or predict which will outperform. It simply holds everything in the index in proportion to each company's size. This approach is called passive investing.

The index itself is maintained by an independent organisation. Companies are added or removed based on predefined rules — for example, the MSCI World adds or removes companies as they cross certain size thresholds. The ETF automatically updates to reflect these changes. You never need to do anything.

Why ETFs Beat Most Alternatives

There are four reasons ETFs have become the default recommendation for beginner investors worldwide:

1. Diversification

When you buy a single company's shares, your investment is entirely dependent on that one company performing well. If it goes bankrupt, you lose everything invested in it. An ETF holding 1,500 companies means no single company can significantly damage your investment. Even if ten companies in your ETF went bankrupt simultaneously — which would be extraordinary — you would still own pieces of the other 1,490.

2. Low Cost

Actively managed funds — where a team of analysts picks stocks on your behalf — typically charge between 1.5% and 2.5% per year in fees. That sounds small, but over 30 years those fees compound just like your returns do, eating a significant portion of your final wealth. ETFs typically charge between 0.1% and 0.5% per year because there is no team of analysts to pay — the fund simply tracks the index automatically.

The Maths on Fees

€10,000 invested for 30 years at 7% annual return with a 0.2% TER grows to ~€72,000. With a 1.5% TER, the same investment grows to only ~€52,000. The fee difference costs you €20,000 on a €10,000 investment.

3. Accessibility

In Germany, you can invest in ETFs through a Sparplan — an automatic monthly investment — with as little as €1 per month on platforms like Trade Republic. There is no minimum lump sum required and no expertise needed to get started.

4. Transparency

You always know exactly what an ETF holds because the index it tracks is publicly available. There are no hidden strategies, no mysterious bets, and no surprises about where your money is going.

The Different Types of ETF

Not all ETFs are the same. Here are the main categories:

  • Equity ETFs — hold shares in companies. These are the most common and the most relevant for long-term growth investors.
  • Bond ETFs — hold government or corporate bonds. Generally lower risk and lower return, often used by investors closer to retirement.
  • Regional ETFs — focus on specific geographies like Europe, US, or Emerging Markets.
  • Sector ETFs — focus on specific industries like technology, healthcare, or clean energy.

For a complete beginner in Germany with a long time horizon, a single broad global equity ETF covers the vast majority of what you need. You do not need multiple ETFs to start.

Accumulating vs Distributing — Important for Germany

This is a detail that specifically matters for German investors and is often overlooked by beginners.

ETFs come in two varieties:

A distributing (ausschüttend) ETF pays dividends — a share of the profits from the companies it holds — directly to you as cash at regular intervals. You receive the money but owe Kapitalertragsteuer on it immediately.

An accumulating (thesaurierend) ETF automatically reinvests those dividends back into the fund. Your investment grows faster because the dividends compound, and in Germany the tax treatment of accumulating ETFs has become more straightforward following the 2018 investment tax reform.

For most young investors in Germany with a long time horizon, accumulating ETFs are generally more tax-efficient because the automatic reinvestment maximises compounding. However tax rules can change and your personal situation matters — this is one area where additional research is worthwhile.

How to Buy an ETF in Germany

You need three things:

  1. A Depot (brokerage account) — The most beginner-friendly options are Trade Republic and Scalable Capital for those 18+, and ING or Comdirect for those under 18 (Minderjährigendepot). See our guide on opening a Depot under 18 in Germany.
  2. Choose your ETF — For a beginner, the iShares Core MSCI World ETF (ISIN: IE00B4L5Y983) is the most commonly recommended starting point. It has a TER of 0.20%, is accumulating, and is available everywhere.
  3. Set up a Sparplan — Decide on a monthly amount, choose your ETF, and set the automatic investment date. From that point it runs without you doing anything.

Also submit a Freistellungsauftrag to your broker when opening your account. This applies your €1,000 annual tax-free allowance (Sparerpauschbetrag) to your investment gains automatically.

The Single Most Important Thing

The best ETF is the one you actually buy. Spending months researching the perfect ETF while doing nothing is far more costly than starting with a reasonable choice today and adjusting later. The MSCI World is an excellent starting point. Start small if you need to — even €10 or €25 per month. The habit and the compounding matter far more than the initial amount.
