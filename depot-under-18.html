Under 18 in Germany

First — Can You Actually Invest Under 18 in Germany?

Yes, you can. But with an important caveat: you cannot open a brokerage account entirely on your own. Under German law, minors (Minderjährige) do not have full legal capacity to enter into financial contracts independently. Your parent or legal guardian must co-sign the account application and give formal consent for the account to be opened in your name.

This is called a Minderjährigendepot — literally a "minor's depot." It is a standard brokerage account held in your name, with parental co-signature confirming their consent and legal responsibility for the account until you turn 18.

Importantly, the money in the account is yours. The parent is not a joint owner — they are simply the legal cosignatory. When you turn 18, the account typically converts automatically to a standard adult Depot in your name alone.

Important Clarification

Not all brokers offer Minderjährigendepots. Many of the popular app-based brokers (Trade Republic, Scalable Capital, Flatex) require customers to be 18+. The options for under-18s are more limited — but they exist, and some are excellent.

Step-by-Step: Opening Your ING Juniordepot

This guide walks through ING as it is the most commonly recommended option for under-18s in Germany. The process at Comdirect is similar.

1

Prepare Your Documents

You will need: your Personalausweis or Reisepass (or a copy), your parent's Personalausweis or Reisepass, your German tax ID (Steueridentifikationsnummer — found on correspondence from the Finanzamt), and your parent's tax ID. Have these ready before starting the application.

2

Go to ING's Website and Start the Application

Navigate to the Juniordepot section on ing.de. Select "Depot eröffnen" and then choose the Juniordepot option. The application is online and takes approximately 15–20 minutes to complete. You will be asked to confirm that a parent or guardian will co-sign.

3

Complete the Identity Verification (Video-Ident)

ING uses a Video-Ident process where you show your ID to a live agent via video call. Both you and your parent must complete this process. This can typically be done same-day via a video call on your phone or computer. The call takes about 5–10 minutes.

4

Wait for Account Activation

After completing Video-Ident, ING processes the application. This typically takes 5–10 business days. You will receive your account details and login credentials by post and email. Do not invest anything until the account is fully activated.

5

Submit Your Freistellungsauftrag Immediately

This is critical and often missed. Log in to your new account and submit a Freistellungsauftrag for €1,000. This tells ING not to withhold Kapitalertragsteuer on your first €1,000 of investment gains each year. If you skip this step, you will pay tax on gains you could have kept. Do it before making any investment.

6

Choose Your ETF and Set Up a Sparplan

Search for the iShares Core MSCI World ETF (ISIN: IE00B4L5Y983) or a similar broad global ETF. Select "Sparplan einrichten," choose your monthly amount (minimum €1 at ING), select your preferred execution date, and confirm. Your first investment will execute on your chosen date and every month thereafter automatically.

7

Fund Your Account

Transfer money to your Depot's reference bank account. ING's Juniordepot is linked to a cash account — transfer your initial amount and any ongoing contributions here. The Sparplan will draw from this account automatically each month, so ensure there is sufficient balance before each execution date.

The Tax Situation for Under-18s in Germany

Understanding German investment tax is important, but it is simpler than it sounds for most young investors.

Kapitalertragsteuer (capital gains tax) is a flat 25% on investment profits, plus a solidarity surcharge (Solidaritätszuschlag), bringing the effective rate to approximately 26.375%. Church tax may also apply if you pay it.

However, the Sparerpauschbetrag — your annual tax-free allowance — means your first €1,000 of investment gains per year are completely tax-free. For most young investors with relatively small portfolios, this means you may pay little to no tax on your investments for many years.

With a Freistellungsauftrag submitted (Step 5 above), your broker automatically applies this allowance, so you do not need to do anything else in most cases. When your portfolio grows large enough that gains exceed €1,000 per year, you will need to handle the tax in your annual Steuererklärung.

One More Thing: The Minderjährigendepot Conversion

When you turn 18, contact your broker. ING and Comdirect both have a straightforward process to convert a Minderjährigendepot to a full adult Depot in your name. The investments stay exactly where they are — nothing is sold, no tax event is triggered. You simply gain full independent control of the account.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to submit the Freistellungsauftrag — costs you unnecessary tax on early gains
  • Choosing a distributing (ausschüttend) ETF when an accumulating one is more tax-efficient for your situation
  • Setting up a Sparplan and then stopping it at the first market dip — compound interest requires staying invested
  • Investing money you might need within the next 1–2 years — only invest money you genuinely will not need for a long time
  • Waiting for the "right time" to start — compound interest rewards starting early, not starting perfectly
