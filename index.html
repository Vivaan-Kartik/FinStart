Quick Reference

Key Terms Explained

Every important finance term, explained in plain language.

ETF
Exchange Traded Fund. A fund that holds many assets (like hundreds of company shares) and trades on a stock exchange. Buying one ETF gives you tiny ownership of everything inside it.
Depot
German word for a brokerage account — the account where you hold your investments. You need one to start investing in Germany.
Sparplan
A German automatic investment plan. You set a fixed monthly amount and the money is invested automatically every month without you doing anything.
MSCI World
An index tracking 1,400+ companies across 23 developed countries. The most commonly recommended starting ETF for German beginners.
TER
Total Expense Ratio. The annual fee an ETF charges. A TER of 0.20% means you pay €2 per year for every €1,000 invested. Lower is better.
Thesaurierend
Accumulating. An accumulating ETF automatically reinvests dividends back into the fund rather than paying them out — maximising compound growth.
Ausschüttend
Distributing. A distributing ETF pays dividends out to you as cash. You then owe Kapitalertragsteuer on those payments.
Kapitalertragsteuer
German capital gains tax. A flat 25% on investment profits plus solidarity surcharge (~26.375% effective). First €1,000/year is tax-free (Sparerpauschbetrag).
Freistellungsauftrag
A form you submit to your broker allowing up to €1,000 of investment gains per year to be tax-free. Always submit this when opening a new Depot.

Why FinStart Exists

Germany has a problem. Despite being one of the most economically sophisticated countries in the world, the average German has minimal exposure to equity markets. Roughly 85% of adults do not own a single share or ETF. The dominant approach to personal finance is to save — carefully, responsibly — and to be deeply suspicious of the stock market.

This is not irrational. German financial culture was shaped by historical hyperinflation, currency collapses, and deep institutional conservatism. But in the modern world of low-cost index funds, the cost of this caution is enormous — measured in tens of thousands of euros of compound growth never earned.

Schools do not teach this. Most personal finance content in German is either patronising, overly complex, or trying to sell you something. I looked for a resource that explained things simply, honestly, and specifically for young Germans starting from zero — and could not find one. So I built it.

Foundations

What is an ETF?
The Complete Beginner's Guide

The simplest investment tool that exists — explained from the ground up. What it is, how it works, why it beats most alternatives, and how to start using one in Germany today.

📖 12 min read🆓 Free🇩🇪 Germany-specific

What Does ETF Actually Stand For?

ETF stands for Exchange Traded Fund. Exchange Traded means it is bought and sold on a stock exchange — the same way you would buy a share in Apple or Volkswagen. Fund means it is a pooled investment vehicle — a collection of many different assets bundled together into one product.

Most ETFs are index funds. This means they track a specific index — a list of companies grouped by certain criteria. An S&P 500 ETF, for example, holds tiny pieces of all 500 companies in the S&P 500 index. When you buy one share of the ETF, you instantly own a proportional slice of all 500 companies simultaneously.

The Key Insight

When you buy one ETF, you own small pieces of hundreds or thousands of companies at once. This is diversification — and it is why ETFs are the default starting point for most beginner investors worldwide.

How ETFs Actually Work

When you invest in an ETF, you are not buying individual shares directly. You are buying units of a fund that holds those shares on your behalf. The ETF manager — a company like iShares (BlackRock), Vanguard, or Xtrackers — buys and holds all the underlying assets and issues units for investors to buy.

The ETF's job is simple: track the performance of its index as closely as possible. It does not try to pick the best companies or predict which will outperform. It simply holds everything in the index in proportion to each company's size. This is called passive investing.

Why ETFs Beat Most Alternatives

1. Diversification

An ETF holding 1,500 companies means no single company can significantly damage your investment. Even if ten companies in your ETF went bankrupt simultaneously, you would still own pieces of the other 1,490.

2. Low Cost

Actively managed funds typically charge 1.5–2.5% per year. ETFs typically charge 0.1–0.5% per year because there is no team of analysts to pay — the fund simply tracks the index automatically.

The Maths on Fees

€10,000 invested for 30 years at 7% return with a 0.2% TER grows to ~€72,000. With a 1.5% TER, the same investment grows to only ~€52,000. The fee difference costs you €20,000 on a €10,000 investment.

3. Accessibility

In Germany, you can invest in ETFs through a Sparplan — an automatic monthly investment — with as little as €1 per month on platforms like Trade Republic. There is no minimum lump sum required.

Annual fee0.1–0.5%1.5–2.5%0%

Accumulating vs Distributing — Important for Germany

A distributing (ausschüttend) ETF pays dividends directly to you as cash. You receive the money but owe Kapitalertragsteuer on it immediately.

An accumulating (thesaurierend) ETF automatically reinvests those dividends back into the fund. Your investment grows faster because the dividends compound. For most young investors in Germany with a long time horizon, accumulating ETFs are generally more tax-efficient.

How to Buy an ETF in Germany

You need three things: (1) a Depot (brokerage account) — see our guide on opening a Depot under 18 in Germany; (2) your chosen ETF — the iShares Core MSCI World ETF (ISIN: IE00B4L5Y983) is the most commonly recommended starting point; (3) a Sparplan — set a monthly amount and let it run automatically.

Also submit a Freistellungsauftrag to your broker immediately. This applies your €1,000 annual tax-free allowance automatically.

The Single Most Important Thing

The best ETF is the one you actually buy. Spending months researching the perfect ETF while doing nothing is far more costly than starting with a reasonable choice today. The MSCI World is an excellent starting point. Start small if you need to — even €10 or €25 per month. The habit and the compounding matter far more than the initial amount.

Compound Interest —
The Most Powerful Concept in Personal Finance

Why starting at 16 is worth dramatically more than starting at 30. The mathematics that will change how you think about money permanently.

📖 10 min read🆓 Free🧮 Real numbers

What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest earned on interest. You invest €1,000 at 7% annual return. After year one you have €1,070. In year two you earn 7% on €1,070 — not your original €1,000. So you end year two with €1,144.90. Year three you earn on €1,144.90. And so it continues, with the base growing slightly larger every single year.

Simple vs Compound — The Critical Difference

With simple interest, €1,000 at 7%/year for 30 years gives you €3,100. With compound interest, the same inputs give you €7,612. The difference — €4,512 — is money you earned without contributing anything extra. That is compound interest at work.

The Ratio That Matters

Of the final €120,965, only 30% came from your own contributions. 70% — nearly three quarters — came from compound growth. The longer you invest, the more dramatic this ratio becomes.

Why Time is the Most Powerful Variable

Two people both invest €100 per month at 7% annual return:

Person A starts at 16 and invests for 50 years (until age 66). Final portfolio: approximately €521,000.

Person B starts at 30 and invests for 36 years (until age 66). Final portfolio: approximately €197,000.

Same monthly amount. Same return rate. Same end age. The only difference is 14 years of starting earlier. The result: a difference of €324,000.

The Rule of 72

Divide 72 by your annual return rate to estimate how long it takes your money to double. At 7% return, 72 ÷ 7 ≈ 10.3 years to double. So €10,000 becomes €20,000 in about 10 years, €40,000 in 20 years, and €80,000 in 30 years — just from compounding, with no additional contributions.

Making This Practical

The most accessible starting point in Germany is a monthly Sparplan into an MSCI World ETF through a platform like Trade Republic or ING. You can start with as little as €1 per month. Set it up, automate it, and let compound interest do its work over decades.

How to Open a Depot
Under 18 in Germany

The complete step-by-step guide. Which brokers accept minors, what documents you need, how to set up a Sparplan, and how to place your first investment.

📖 14 min read🆓 Free🇩🇪 Germany-specific

Can You Actually Invest Under 18 in Germany?

Yes — but with an important caveat. Under German law, minors cannot open a financial account entirely on their own. Your parent or legal guardian must co-sign the account application. This is called a Minderjährigendepot. The money is yours — the parent is simply the legal cosignatory. When you turn 18, the account converts automatically to a standard adult Depot.

Important

Not all brokers offer Minderjährigendepots. Many popular app-based brokers (Trade Republic, Scalable Capital, Flatex) require customers to be 18+. The options for under-18s are more limited — but they exist, and some are excellent.

Which Brokers Accept Customers Under 18

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
ING

Offers a Juniordepot. Well-established bank, reliable app, good ETF selection. Sparpläne from €1. Generally considered the best option for under-18s.

⭐⭐⭐⭐
Comdirect

Strong option with Minderjährigendepot. Part of Commerzbank group. Slightly more complex interface but full functionality.

⭐⭐⭐
DKB

Deutsche Kreditbank offers youth accounts with investing functionality. Requires a DKB bank account.

Step-by-Step: Opening Your ING Juniordepot

1

Prepare Your Documents

You will need: your Personalausweis or Reisepass (or a copy), your parent's Personalausweis or Reisepass, your German tax ID (Steueridentifikationsnummer), and your parent's tax ID.

2

Start the Application on ING's Website

Navigate to the Juniordepot section on ing.de. Select "Depot eröffnen" and choose the Juniordepot option. The application is online and takes approximately 15–20 minutes.

3

Complete Video Identity Verification

ING uses a Video-Ident process where you show your ID to a live agent via video call. Both you and your parent must complete this process. The call takes about 5–10 minutes.

4

Wait for Account Activation

Processing typically takes 5–10 business days. You will receive your account details by post and email. Do not invest anything until the account is fully activated.

5

Submit Your Freistellungsauftrag Immediately

Log in and submit a Freistellungsauftrag for €1,000. This tells ING not to withhold Kapitalertragsteuer on your first €1,000 of investment gains each year. Do this before making any investment.

6

Choose Your ETF and Set Up a Sparplan

Search for the iShares Core MSCI World ETF (ISIN: IE00B4L5Y983). Select "Sparplan einrichten," choose your monthly amount (minimum €1 at ING), select your execution date, and confirm. It runs automatically from that point.

7

Fund Your Account

Transfer money to your Depot's reference bank account. The Sparplan will draw from this account automatically each month — ensure there is sufficient balance before each execution date.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to submit the Freistellungsauftrag — costs you unnecessary tax on early gains
  • Choosing a distributing ETF when an accumulating one is more tax-efficient
  • Stopping your Sparplan at the first market dip — compound interest requires staying invested
  • Investing money you might need within the next 1–2 years
  • Waiting for the "right time" to start — there is no right time, just now
Educational disclaimer: Broker features, fees, and processes change regularly — always verify current information directly with your chosen broker before opening an account. This does not constitute financial advice.

The MSCI World ETF —
Everything You Need to Know

Why this single index fund is the most commonly recommended starting point. What it contains, how it has performed, and what you should know before investing.

What is the MSCI World?

The MSCI World is a stock market index maintained by MSCI Inc. It tracks large and mid-sized companies across 23 developed countries, making it one of the broadest and most diversified equity indices available. Buy one unit of an MSCI World ETF and you own small pieces of over 1,400 companies across North America, Europe, Asia-Pacific, and beyond.

Important Context

The MSCI World does not include emerging markets — countries like China, India, or Brazil. For exposure to those, you would need a separate Emerging Markets ETF. Many beginner investors start with just the MSCI World.

Historical Performance

Over the past 30+ years, the MSCI World has delivered an average annual return of approximately 7–8% in EUR terms. This includes several major crashes — the dot-com crash (2000–2002, approximately -48%), the global financial crisis (2008–2009, approximately -55%), and the COVID crash (2020, approximately -33%). Each crash fully recovered and the index went on to new all-time highs.

Which MSCI World ETF to Choose

  • iShares Core MSCI World ETF (ISIN: IE00B4L5Y983) — TER 0.20%, accumulating, very large fund (~€50bn+). The most commonly held MSCI World ETF in Germany. Available as a Sparplan from €1 at most brokers.
  • Xtrackers MSCI World Swap ETF (ISIN: LU0274208692) — TER 0.19%, accumulating. Slightly cheaper, equally reputable.
  • Vanguard FTSE All-World ETF (ISIN: IE00B3RBWM25) — TER 0.22%, includes emerging markets. A slightly broader option.

Criticisms and Limitations

Heavy US concentration: With roughly 67% in the United States, the MSCI World is not truly "global." If US markets underperform significantly, an MSCI World investor will feel the full impact.

No emerging markets: Countries like China, India, and Brazil are excluded despite containing enormous and growing economies.

The Practical Conclusion

For a beginner with a long time horizon, the MSCI World ETF remains an excellent starting point despite its imperfections. The combination of broad diversification, low cost, and accessibility through German brokers makes it the default recommendation in most beginner finance education worldwide.

Saving vs Investing —
What's the Difference?

Why these two things are fundamentally different, why confusing them costs people a fortune over a lifetime, and exactly which one to use when.

📖 9 min read🆓 Free🏦 Fundamentals

Why Germany Gets This Wrong

Germany has a deeply ingrained savings culture. And saving is good. But Germany is also the country where roughly 85% of adults do not own a single share or ETF. Many Germans save diligently and invest almost nothing — keeping large sums in Tagesgeldkonten earning 1–3% while inflation quietly erodes the real value of their wealth over decades.

Saving vs Investing — The Core Difference

💶 Saving

Low-risk, accessible accounts
Returns are low but guaranteed
Capital is protected — no nominal loss
Suitable for short time horizons
No real growth beyond inflation

📈 Investing

Assets that can grow in value over time
Returns are higher but variable
Capital is at risk — value can fall
Suitable for long time horizons (5+ years)
Real long-term growth significantly exceeding inflation

When to Save

  • Your emergency fund — Keep 3–6 months of living expenses in a liquid, accessible account at all times.
  • Short-term goals (under 3–5 years) — If you need the money within a few years, that money belongs in savings.
  • Money you cannot afford to lose — Any amount where a 30–40% temporary loss would cause genuine hardship should not be in equities.

When to Invest

Investing is appropriate when: you have an emergency fund in place; the money will not be needed for at least 5 years; and you can emotionally tolerate the money declining in value for 1–3 years without selling in panic.

The Simple Rule

Save for the short term. Invest for the long term. Keep 3–6 months of expenses in savings always. Everything else that you will not need for 5+ years — invest it. This one rule, consistently applied, is more valuable than most complex financial strategies.

The Hidden Cost of Only Saving

When prices rise 2.5% per year and your savings account pays 1.5%, you are losing 1% of your purchasing power every year in real terms. After 20 years, €10,000 in a 1.5% savings account with 2.5% inflation is worth only about €8,183 in today's money. You have lost nearly €1,817 of real wealth while thinking you were saving carefully.
