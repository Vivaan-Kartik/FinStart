What is the MSCI World?

The MSCI World is a stock market index maintained by MSCI Inc. (Morgan Stanley Capital International). It tracks large and mid-sized companies across 23 developed countries, making it one of the broadest and most diversified equity indices available.

When people say "MSCI World ETF," they mean an ETF that tracks this index — holding proportional shares in every company inside it. Buy one unit of an MSCI World ETF and you own small pieces of over 1,400 companies across North America, Europe, Asia-Pacific, and beyond.

What's Actually Inside the MSCI World?

The MSCI World is heavily weighted toward the United States, which dominates global equity markets by market capitalisation. The approximate regional breakdown looks like this:

RegionApprox. WeightKey Countries
North America~70%USA (~67%), Canada (~3%)
Europe~15%UK, France, Germany, Switzerland, Sweden
Asia-Pacific~12%Japan (~6%), Australia, Hong Kong, Singapore
Other Developed~3%Israel, New Zealand

The largest individual holdings are the biggest companies in the world by market capitalisation — companies like Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Nvidia, Alphabet (Google), and Meta. These US tech giants alone represent a significant portion of the total index weight.

Important Context

The MSCI World does not include emerging markets — countries like China, India, Brazil, or South Korea. For exposure to those, you would need a separate Emerging Markets ETF, or a combined product like the MSCI All Country World Index (ACWI). Many beginner investors start with just the MSCI World and consider adding emerging markets later.

Historical Performance

Past performance does not guarantee future results — but historical context is useful for understanding what to expect from this type of investment.

Over the past 30+ years, the MSCI World has delivered an average annual return of approximately 7–8% in EUR terms (varying depending on the specific period measured and currency). This includes several major crashes: the dot-com crash (2000–2002, approximately -48%), the global financial crisis (2008–2009, approximately -55%), and the COVID crash (2020, approximately -33% peak-to-trough before a rapid recovery).

Each of these crashes fully recovered within a few years and the index went on to new all-time highs. This is the core argument for staying invested through downturns rather than selling in panic — historically, patience has been rewarded.

That said, there is no guarantee that future decades will resemble past ones. An investor beginning today should be aware that returns in any given decade could be significantly higher or lower than the historical average.

Which MSCI World ETF to Choose

Multiple ETF providers offer funds tracking the MSCI World. The main options available in Germany are:

  • iShares Core MSCI World ETF (ISIN: IE00B4L5Y983) — TER 0.20%, accumulating, domiciled in Ireland, very large fund size (~€50bn+). The most commonly held MSCI World ETF in Germany. Sparplan available at most brokers from €1.
  • Xtrackers MSCI World Swap ETF (ISIN: LU0274208692) — TER 0.19%, accumulating, uses synthetic replication. Slightly cheaper, equally reputable.
  • Vanguard FTSE All-World ETF (ISIN: IE00B3RBWM25) — TER 0.22%, distributing version available. Note: this tracks the FTSE All-World index (which includes emerging markets) rather than the MSCI World specifically, but is often mentioned alongside MSCI World products.

For a beginner, the iShares Core MSCI World (IE00B4L5Y983) is a perfectly sound default choice. The differences between these products are small enough that the choice of which one matters far less than simply starting.

Criticisms and Limitations

The MSCI World is not perfect, and understanding its limitations makes you a better investor.

Heavy US concentration

With roughly 67% allocated to the United States, the MSCI World is not truly "global" in the way the name implies. If US markets underperform significantly over the coming decades, an MSCI World investor will feel the full impact. Some investors partially address this with a tilt toward European or emerging market exposure.

No emerging markets

Countries like China, India, and Brazil are excluded despite containing enormous and growing economies. Whether the higher growth potential of emerging markets justifies their higher risk is a genuine ongoing debate among investors.

Market-cap weighting amplifies the largest companies

Because the index weights companies by market capitalisation, a few enormous tech companies dominate the top holdings. This means the MSCI World is already highly concentrated in US technology — a point worth understanding, even if it does not necessarily change what you should do.

The Practical Conclusion

For a beginner with a long time horizon, the MSCI World ETF remains an excellent starting point despite its imperfections. The combination of broad diversification, low cost, accessibility through German brokers, and decades of historical data makes it the default recommendation in most beginner finance education worldwide. Its limitations are worth knowing — but they are unlikely to be reasons not to start with it.
