Quick Reference

Key Terms Explained

Every important finance term, explained in plain language. No jargon, no assumptions.

ETF
Exchange Traded Fund. A fund that holds many assets (like hundreds of company shares) and trades on a stock exchange. Buying one ETF gives you tiny ownership of everything inside it.
Depot
German word for a brokerage account — the account where you hold your investments such as ETFs and shares. You need one to start investing in Germany.
Sparplan
A German automatic investment plan. You set a fixed monthly amount, choose what to invest in, and the money is invested automatically every month without you doing anything.
MSCI World
An index tracking 1,500+ companies across 23 developed countries. Often used as the basis for beginner ETFs because it provides broad global diversification in a single fund.
TER
Total Expense Ratio. The annual fee an ETF charges, expressed as a percentage. A TER of 0.20% means you pay €2 per year for every €1,000 invested. Lower is better.
Thesaurierend
German for "accumulating." An accumulating ETF automatically reinvests dividends back into the fund rather than paying them out — maximising compound growth.
Ausschüttend
German for "distributing." A distributing ETF pays dividends out to you as cash. You then owe Kapitalertragsteuer on those payments in Germany.
Kapitalertragsteuer
German capital gains tax. A flat 25% tax on investment profits plus solidarity surcharge, making the effective rate ~26.375%. The first €1,000/year is tax-free (Sparerpauschbetrag).
Freistellungsauftrag
A form you submit to your broker allowing up to €1,000 of investment gains per year to be tax-free. Always submit this when opening a new Depot in Germany.
Dividende
A cash payment companies make to shareholders from their profits. ETFs either pay these out (ausschüttend) or reinvest them automatically (thesaurierend).
Volatilität
Volatility. How much an investment's price fluctuates up and down. Higher volatility means bigger swings in value — typically associated with higher potential returns but also higher risk.
Rebalancing
Periodically adjusting your portfolio back to your target allocation. If stocks rise and become a larger share of your portfolio than intended, rebalancing means selling some stocks and buying more bonds.
