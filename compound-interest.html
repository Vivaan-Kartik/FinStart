What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest earned on interest. That definition sounds circular, so let us make it concrete immediately.

You invest €1,000 at 7% annual return. After year one you have €1,070. In year two you do not earn 7% on your original €1,000 — you earn 7% on €1,070. So you end year two with €1,144.90. Year three you earn on €1,144.90. And so it continues, with the base growing slightly larger every single year.

The interest earns interest. Every year. Indefinitely. This is the core of compound interest — and over long periods, the mathematics becomes extraordinary.

Simple vs Compound — The Critical Difference

With simple interest, €1,000 at 7%/year for 30 years gives you €1,000 + (€70 × 30) = €3,100. With compound interest, the same inputs give you €7,612. The difference — €4,512 — is money you earned without contributing anything extra. That is compound interest at work.

The Ratio That Matters

Of the final €120,965, only 30% came from your own contributions. 70% — nearly three quarters of the final amount — came from compound growth. The longer you invest, the more dramatic this ratio becomes.

Why Time Is The Most Powerful Variable

Compound interest is not magic. It is mathematics — and the key variable is time. The longer your money compounds, the more dramatic the effect. This is why starting young is not just good advice — it is quantifiably, mathematically transformative.

Here is the comparison that matters most for you specifically. Two people both invest €100 per month at 7% annual return:

Person A starts at 16 and invests for 50 years (until age 66). Final portfolio: approximately €521,000.

Person B starts at 30 and invests for 36 years (until age 66). Final portfolio: approximately €197,000.

Same monthly amount. Same return rate. Same end age. The only difference is 14 years of starting earlier. The result is a difference of €324,000.

Person A contributed €14 × 12 × 100 = €16,800 more than Person B over their lifetime. But they ended up with €324,000 more. That is because those early contributions had decades longer to compound — and in compound interest, the early years are disproportionately powerful.

The Rule of 72

A quick way to estimate how long it takes your money to double: divide 72 by your annual return rate. At 7% return, 72 ÷ 7 = approximately 10.3 years to double. So €10,000 becomes €20,000 in about 10 years, €40,000 in 20 years, and €80,000 in 30 years — just from compounding, with no additional contributions.

Why Your Savings Account is Losing Money

Compound interest works in both directions. If you keep money in a savings account earning 1% per year while inflation runs at 2.5% per year, your money is experiencing negative compound growth in real terms.

After 10 years, €10,000 in a 1% savings account has grown to €11,046. But with 2.5% annual inflation, that €11,046 only buys what €8,700 would have bought 10 years ago. You have lost purchasing power despite earning interest. This is the silent cost of avoiding investment — not dramatic loss, but invisible, steady erosion.

Compound interest working in your favour through investing generates wealth. Compound interest working against you through inflation and inaction destroys it. The choice of which direction it works is largely determined by what you do with your money.

Making This Practical — What to Do Right Now

The mathematics only matters if it changes your behaviour. Here is what compound interest practically means for a 16-year-old in Germany:

Every month you wait to start investing costs you compound growth. Not in a catastrophic way — but in a permanent, irreversible way. The €100 you invest at 16 has 50 years to compound. The €100 you invest at 17 has 49 years. The difference sounds small — but at 7%, one additional year of compounding is worth roughly 7% of the entire final value of that contribution.

The most accessible starting point in Germany is a monthly Sparplan into an MSCI World ETF through a platform like Trade Republic or ING. You can start with as little as €1 per month. The amount matters far less than the habit. Set it up, automate it, and let compound interest do its work over decades.
